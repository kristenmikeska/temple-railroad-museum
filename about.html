Our Story

The City the Railroad Built

For more than 50 years, the Temple Railroad & Heritage Museum has preserved the rich history of how one railroad changed Central Texas forever — and built a city in a single afternoon.

Our Mission

To inspire visitors to discover our rich railroad heritage and the history that shaped our community — and to preserve it for generations to come.

Our Vision

To be the educational destination for our community to explore its past and present, and a national research resource for Santa Fe Railroad and Texas railroad history.

Our Values

We are guided by Education, Excellence, Integrity, Service, and Stewardship — the same values that drove the railroad workers who built this region.

The Founding

A City Born at a Junction

On June 29, 1881, two major railways — the Gulf, Colorado & Santa Fe Railway and the Missouri-Kansas-Texas Railroad (known as "The Katy") — converged at what became Temple Junction. Through an auction of 157 business lots and 28 residential lots, a new settlement took shape on the Texas prairie, named in honor of Bernard Moore Temple, the Santa Fe Railway's chief engineer.

"Temple sprang up almost overnight — within just three years, the frontier settlement had 3,000 residents, three churches, a school, two banks, and two newspapers."

The railroad didn't just found Temple. It shaped every aspect of life here. In 1891, the Gulf, Colorado & Santa Fe opened Temple's first hospital specifically to care for its workers — the same institution that would grow into today's Baylor Scott & White Health, one of the largest medical systems in Texas.

Temple grew quickly into one of the region's most vital rail hubs. Early residents called it "Mudtown" for its unpaved streets — but no one called it quiet. The trains never stopped coming, and neither did the people who followed them.

The Building

A Landmark Brought Back to Life

The Santa Fe Depot was completed in 1911 as the Southern Division Headquarters of the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway. At its peak, it was one of the most important rail facilities in Central Texas — the place where passengers arrived, workers reported, and the commerce of the region passed through.

When the railroad vacated the building in the 1990s, it could have been lost forever. Instead, the people of Temple chose to invest $4 million — combining a federal transportation grant with city funds — to restore it. Construction began in 1999 and the fully restored depot reopened in 2000.

Today the depot stands adjacent to an active BNSF and Amtrak rail yard, so visitors can still hear and see trains passing — the same way Temple's first residents did 140 years ago.

What Guides Us

Our Core Values

Education

Every exhibit, program, and tour is an opportunity to learn — for students, families, and lifelong learners alike.

Excellence

We hold ourselves to the highest standards in preservation, programming, and the visitor experience.

Integrity

We tell history honestly and completely — the hard parts alongside the triumphs — because that is what history deserves.

Service

We serve our visitors, our community, and the historical record with equal dedication and care.

Stewardship

We are guardians of irreplaceable artifacts, archives, and a building that belongs to the people of Temple.

Housed in the historic 1911 Santa Fe Depot, the museum preserves the railroad heritage and community history of Temple, Texas and all of Central Texas.
